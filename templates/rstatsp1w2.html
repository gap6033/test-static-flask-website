  • S.D is also a measure of spread used in symmetrical distribution. IQR is a better measure of spread in skewed distribution

Data Visualization with R
  • Descriptive Statistics - With descriptive statistics, we get to literally explain to somebody else the parameters or the characteristics that make up a numerical or quantitative variable. It tells what the distribution of a variable looks like.Skewed data means more variation.Symmetric data means less variation
  • Import the Dataset
  • header(datasetname) gives you a preview of few rows(first 6) with all the variables in the console. This will help you to know if the variable you want to plot is categorical or quantitative.
  • plot(datasetname$variable)....plot is used in case of categorical variables. This will give you a bar chart representation in the work environment(window in the bottom right corner) but without any x-axis or y-axis value or a heading for the graph. To get these values; - (for heading)main='whatever heading you want', xlab ='whatever x axis represents', ylab = 'whatever...'. In summary plot(datasetname$variable,main='whatever heading you want', xlab ='whatever x axis represents', ylab = 'whatever...')
  • For quantitative variable - a histogram replace "plot" with "hist"
  • When you want to work with a variable within a sub-dataset of a dataset, use assignment to reach that variable within the sub-dataset and then depending what the type of variable is use plot or hist and inside the parenthesis mention the assignment name
  • femaledogsage <- datasetname$ageofintake[datasetname$gender == 'Female'] hist(femaledogsage)
  • Specifying the number of bins - breaks (convention to keep bins between 10-15), works with "plot" also hist(femaledogsage, breaks = 5), will have 5 bins/5 bars hist(femaledogsage, breaks = 15), will have 15
  • max(assignmentname) works with quantitative variable only and will give the maximum value present for that variable for which the assignment was created then you can use (the main datasetname not the subdataset) datasetname[row number,].....nothing after"," as we want to view all other variables for that max value.
  • Calculating Center and Spread (as obvious, relevant only for quantitative variable)-
    • mean(datasetname$variable)
    • median(datasetname$variable)
    • sd(datasetname$variable)
    • fivenum(datasetname$variable)
Z-score
  • The z-score table gives the probability of the left side of a normal distribution. If you want to know the probability of the right side and you have your z-score (which would obviously be positive since its on the right half) just change the sign of z-score to negative and look at the corresponding probability. That would be the answer.
